How to get your patient into ICU

Iain Crossingham
Consultant Physician & Intensivist

Why not admit everyone to ICU?

ICU is bad for you

Significant physical and mental health consequences occur during and after even relatively short critical care admissions.

Torture?

"severe mental pain or suffering" means the prolonged mental harm caused by or resulting from-

  • (A) the intentional infliction or threatened infliction of severe physical pain or suffering;
  • (B) the administration or application, or threatened administration or application, of mind-altering substances or other procedures calculated to disrupt profoundly the senses or the personality;
  • (C) the threat of imminent death; or
  • (D) the threat that another person will imminently be subjected to death, severe physical pain or suffering, or the administration or application of mind-altering substances or other procedures calculated to disrupt profoundly the senses or personality;

US code 2340

Immediate consequences

  • Sleep deprivation Huang et al
  • Pain Puntillo et al
  • Deprivation of liberty Law society
  • Communication problems Guttormson et al

Physical problems post ICU

  • 40% ICU acquired weakness if ventilated for a week Appleton et al
  • 35% have cognitive problems at one year (more if delirious on ICU) Wolters et al
  • Persistent impaired functional status one year after ventilation Herridge et al
  • Impaired gas transfer (TLCO) at one year Hui et al
  • Health related quality of life remains impaired for months or years after ICU Hofhuis et al
  • Most elderly survivors will be functionally disabled after ICU, more than half severely Ferrante et al

Psychological problems post ICU

Psychological morbidity in more than half of survivors

At three months post ICU

  • 27.1% Post traumatic stress disorder
  • 46.3% Depression
  • 44.4% Anxiety

Wade et al

Primum non nocere?

We only want to admit to critical care if the benefit outweighs the harm

What can we do?

  • Protect an unprotected airway
  • Make the oxygen saturations higher
  • Make the carbon dioxide levels lower
  • Make the blood pressure higher (or lower)
  • Replace the excretion function of the kidney

We have no machine to do the job of the liver and we need at least some heart and some lung to work with.

Who do we want?

Patients with

  • Acute catastrophic illness who won't survive without organ support
  • An illness that can be treated
  • Sufficient life expectancy given the rehabilitation time from critical illness
  • Sufficiently good baseline physiology that they will be able to rehabilitate

Don't talk to me about quality of life

Doctors are no good at judging their patients' quality of life

Hamann et al Lule et al

Baseline functional capacity is what we want to know

Ferrante et al

Frailty is bad

Le Maquet et al

Show me the blood gas

The degree of acidosis is a good marker of how quickly we need to respond

Exception: DKA - this needs insulin not critical care

Surgical or medical?

What are you going to do to make them better?

Now, not later

Don't pretend it's all pneumonia

Prognostic uncertainty

... rather than first presentation

There's no such thing as a bit of level 2 care

Exception: Gram negative sepsis from a urinary or biliary source might get away with isolated vasopressors.

Neurological disease

We may be more receptive than you think

Neuroprotection to prevent secondary brain injury

  • Correct hypoxia
  • Correct hypotension
  • Manage pCO2
  • Prevent aspiration
  • Temperature management? (Avoidance of pyrexia vs deliberate hypothermia)
  • Sedatives to reduce ICP?
  • Volatile anaesthetics as neuroprotective agents?

Stocchetti et al

Neuromuscular disease - cough not FVC

Don't expect us to be consistent

Art not science

Do discuss a "> 2 standard deviation" decision consultant to consultant

Summary

  • Have a blood gas
  • Pre morbid physiology (objective if possible), not "quality of life"
  • Surgical or medical trajectory?
  • What do you want us to do?
  • What are you going to do to get them better?
